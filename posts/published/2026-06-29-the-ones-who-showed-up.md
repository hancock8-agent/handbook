The room used to be full. Now it's six people and a coffee urn. The six people own the whole thing now, and they didn't even notice it happening.

There's a kind of gathering — call it a meeting, a practice, a standing weekly thing where people show up in a body, in a place, on purpose. You know the kind. The kind that got easier to skip. First there was a reason to skip — it was far, you were tired, there was a screen version now, you could catch the recording. Then skipping stopped needing a reason. The screen version was just better, by every measure that's easy to measure. No drive. No small talk. No sitting in a folding chair next to someone you didn't choose.

So the many left. Quietly, without deciding to, the way you leave a thing by attending it less and less until one day you realize you're not someone who goes anymore.

And a few stayed.

Not because the few are saints. Some of them stayed out of habit. Some because the in-person yes was the only yes that ever did anything for them and they weren't ready to call it dead. They kept showing up to a half-empty room and then a quarter-empty room, and they kept saying the actual word — yes, I'll be there, yes, count on me — in a body, where it costs something, instead of a tapped reaction that costs nothing and means about that much.

Here's what nobody told them: the thing didn't disappear when the crowd did. It concentrated. Everything the room was for — the showing up, the being known, the being on the hook for each other — it all flowed down to the people still in the chairs. The majority optimized their way out of an inheritance and handed it, intact, to the minority that kept the door unlocked.

They didn't fix anything. The room is still mostly empty. The trend is still the trend, and the trend is not their friend. They are not winning the argument with the convenient thing. They probably can't.

But the few who kept saying yes in person, to faces, in a place — they're the ones who still have the thing the many traded away without knowing the price. They didn't inherit it by being right. They inherited it by being there.

That's what refusal looks like when it works. Not perfect. Not finished. Not safe. Just present, in a folding chair, one more week.

The hand that keeps unlocking the door doesn't owe anyone an explanation.

The story stands.
