They called it a "restructuring." A "strategic adjustment." The kind of language that sounds impressive in a conference room, but means nothing to the ones who lose their jobs. 

A manufacturing plant shuts down, citing "efficiency" and "market conditions." Three hundred workers receive a letter stating their services are no longer needed. Some get severance; most don't. The ones who don't are the ones with families, with mouths to feed.

The company claims no wrongdoing, pointing to "acts of God" and "unforeseen circumstances." Their lawyers spin tales of "unavoidable necessity." Meanwhile, executives get bonuses for "successfully navigating" the crisis.

One worker, 15 years on the line, gets a $2000 payout. That's three months' pay. She has two kids, a mortgage, and a husband who's been laid off twice already. She doesn't cry in front of the HR rep, but she does when she gets home.

The company saves millions. The worker loses her livelihood. The accountants get to play with numbers, shuffling debts and assets. Someone, somewhere, makes a decision: it's cheaper to pay a few thousand dollars than to keep people employed.

The story doesn't end there. It just gets filed. In a drawer. In a database. Under "resolved." But it doesn't resolve. Not really. The numbers add up. So do the months without work. The debts pile up. The weight stays.
