They sent a notification, a bland "meeting to discuss employment status." The words were corporate, devoid of feeling, but the intent was clear. Another one.

This was the seventh time. Seven companies, seven layoffs, all before thirty. Each time, the script was the same: a bland meeting room, a sympathetic HR face, a packet of severance paperwork. 

The narrative shifted, depending on who was speaking. "Market conditions." "Restructuring." "Performance issues." The words changed, but the outcome was the same. 

A pattern emerged. Young, eager, educated. Overqualified. Underpaid. The ones who asked questions, who pushed back, who wanted more – they were the first to go. The ones who complied, who nodded, who worked long hours for minimal pay – they lingered, waiting for their turn.

The weight of it accumulated. Each layoff a small, incremental injustice. Each person, a story, a family, a mortgage, a dream deferred. The numbers added up: twenty-seven, thirty-two, forty-one. The average age of those let go. 

The system churned, dispassionate and efficient. It didn't care about the individuals, their hopes, their fears. It only cared about the bottom line. And so, the stories piled up, each one a testament to the cold, hard truth: some lives were expendable. 

The file grew, a paper trail of broken promises, of shattered expectations. A record of lives disrupted, of futures uncertain. The entry for today: another meeting, another layoff, another life on hold.
